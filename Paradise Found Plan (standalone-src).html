A Plan for Paradise

A work-exchange contribution plan — supporting, not restructuring, the farm Warren has already built

📍 Sierra Vista / Bisbee, AZ
📅 Arrival: May 23rd
🌧️ Monsoon: Late June onset
🌿 Observation before intervention
My Approach

Four Guiding Principles

This is your farm. My role is to add value within the systems you've built — quietly, carefully, and only after I understand how they work.

01

Support, Don't Restructure

Improve and extend existing farm operations rather than replacing or reorganizing them. The systems already in place have been earned through years of work — my job is to make them stronger, not different.

02

Focus the Work

Concentrate effort on four high-leverage areas: soil health, water retention, animal systems, and nursery fertility. Everything else is secondary until these are stable and improving.

03

Small, High-Impact Improvements

Enhance the visitor experience through low-cost, high-yield changes — clearer flow, better signage, small habitat touches — before considering anything that requires capital or rebuilding.

04

Learn Before Proposing

Spend the first weeks reading the land, the systems, and the workflow. No large-scale proposals until I've watched a full monsoon cycle and understood why things are done the way they are.

The phase plan below is a map of intent, not a contract. Every proposed action is contingent on what the first month of observation reveals.

🔭
Phase 1 · Days 0–30

Observe Before Intervening

The most important thing I can do in the first month is watch and listen — to the land, the animals, the water, and the visitors. Every observation before the monsoon hits is data I cannot get after the fact.

Days 0–30
Critical Timing Note Arrival is late May. Monsoon onset is late June. This gives a narrow window to map hydrology before the first rains arrive. Month 1 is about reading the land in its dry-season state — before disrupting anything. No major earthworks until rainfall confirms the flow paths.
💧
Water Systems
Hydrology Mapping
▾
  • Map water movement patterns after the first rains arrive
  • Identify erosion zones and dry runoff channels across the property
  • Locate natural low points that indicate future pond sites
  • Observe where existing plantings (mulberry, fig, blackberry) collect vs. shed water
  • Document topography sketches for swale placement planning
Why this first Flat windy desert is deceptive. Without watching one rain event, you cannot know where the water wants to go. Build swales on the wrong contour and you've done expensive harm.
🌱
Soil Systems
Baseline Assessment
▾
  • Informal soil sampling across 4–5 zones — texture, color, compaction, biology
  • Audit existing goat manure handling and curing process
  • Assess what compost infrastructure is already present or improvised
  • Identify the fastest wins: where can goat manure go directly, and where does it need processing?
  • Observe plant stress signals in the nursery as indicators of soil health
🐐
Animal Systems
Welfare + Flow Audit
▾
  • Observe animal stress points in the current barn-based petting zoo setup
  • Map traffic patterns: where do visitors cluster, where do they avoid, where do they linger?
  • Note shade availability, water access, and animal separation options
  • Assess emu containment in context of expanded visitor access
  • Identify low-cost enrichment opportunities for existing animals
👤
Visitor Experience
Friction Point Mapping
▾
  • Walk the guest experience as a visitor — note what feels incomplete or confusing
  • Identify missing signage, unclear pathways, and photo opportunity gaps
  • Assess the entry sequence: arrival → parking → first impression → petting zoo
  • Note where guests slow down vs. move through quickly
  • Review HipCamp and Airbnb listings against the actual on-ground experience
Quick Win Opportunity $20 entry is underpriced for the experience described. Before building the dedicated enclosure, small flow improvements and a clear feeding station structure could justify a $30–35 price point immediately.
Deliverable 1
Full systems map of farm as-is
Deliverable 2

Deliverable 3
Animal welfare + visitor flow report
Deliverable 4
Compost/manure baseline assessment

Phase 2 · Days 30–90

Light Earthworks + System Seeding

With one monsoon observation cycle complete, we begin shaping the land. Light earthworks aligned with observed water flow. Soil biology introduced. Candidate animals for expansion brought in for experience value and system function.

Days 30–90
⛏️
Earthworks
Water Harvesting Begins
▾
  • Contour swales — only after rainfall observation confirms actual flow paths
  • Half-moon infiltration basins around existing orchard zones (fig, mulberry, pomegranate)
  • Berm reinforcement near nursery water zones to reduce runoff loss
  • Passive water redirection toward identified future pond site
Design Principle The rock circles Warren already built around his orchard trees are intuitive water harvesting. This phase extends that logic across the property systematically.
🧪
Soil Biology
Fertility System Introduction
▾
  • Indigenous Microorganism (IMO) culture from on-site forest floor — native biology only
  • Comfrey planted as Fermented Plant Juice (FPJ) source — growth hormones and enzymes
  • Goat manure stream segmented: direct application vs. hot compost vs. fungal habitat
  • Wood chip fungal habitat zones laid in orchard and nursery areas
  • Three-bin compost system established with labeled streams
🌊
Water System
Pond Site Preparation
▾
  • First pond excavation — primary capture, habitat, and future duck system
  • Pond-integrated ecology design: spillways that direct overflow to orchard zones
  • Biochar staging in pond edge zones for nutrient activation
  • Compost tea brewing using pond water — closed-loop inoculation
For the Ducks Ducks need a water feature that doesn't evaporate in a week or go stagnant. A properly sited earthen pond with spillways solves this — and adds habitat, water storage, and Year 2 chinampa potential simultaneously.
🐾

Value Chain
Compost + Manure Product Line
▾

The farm's waste streams become branded soil products — an internal revenue expansion with minimal new input cost.

Input Streams
  • Goat manure — existing high-volume base
  • Chicken manure — high nitrogen amendment
  • Emu manure — dry bulk structure
  • Rabbit manure — direct-use nursery grade
  • Duck manure — once pond system is active
Branded Outputs
  • Desert Regeneration Blend — primary compost product
  • Nursery Inoculate — high-nutrient fast-turnover mix
  • Orchard Restore — fungal-dominant slow-release
  • Pond-Aged Biochar — premium amendment tier

Phase 3 · Days 90–120

Integration + Experience Scaling

The regenerative systems built in Phase 2 are now visible and functioning. Phase 3 turns them into the guest experience — making the ecological work legible, beautiful, and worth paying for.

Days 90–120
🗺️
Guest Experience
Desert Ecology Loop
▾

A self-guided or hosted walking experience that shows visitors how the farm works as a living system:

  • Animals — the heart of the experience, now in dedicated habitat zones
  • Compost Station — visible, labeled, and explainable to curious guests
  • Nursery + Orchard — fruit trees with visible half-moon water basins and plant ID signs
  • Pond System — ducks, ecology, water story; the visual anchor of the loop
The Shift Visitors don't just see animals. They see how the land sustains itself. That story is worth $10–15 more per ticket and dramatically increases repeat visits and word-of-mouth referrals.
🏡
Petting Zoo Redesign
Barn → Habitat Zones
▾
  • Tortoise habitat zone — slow ecology / education anchor
  • Duck pond system — water ecology, feeding interaction
  • Mini donkey loop — load-bearing personality animals, walkalong experience
  • Peacock corridor — aesthetic attraction layer, photography zone
  • Goat + emu core area — existing anchor system, enhanced flow and shade
Pricing Implication A dedicated enclosure with clear entry, feeding station, and habitat zones supports $30–35/entry. Add a premium "farm ecology tour" at $55–65/person for groups. The emus alone sell the experience.
🌱

  • Mulberry — shade + fodder + visitor canopy system

Long Term · Month 4+

The Closed-Loop Farm

The goal beyond 120 days: a farm where every system feeds every other system, waste is invisible, and visitors can see the land sustaining itself. This is not complexity — it is visible ecological function that increases authenticity and revenue simultaneously.

Month 4+
♾️
System Design
Four Interconnected Loops
▾
🐐 Animal Loop
Feed → Manure → Soil → Feed
Every animal on the property contributes to fertility. No waste stream leaves the land.
🌱 Compost Loop
Waste → Inoculation → Nursery → Sale
Branded soil products created from what was previously being managed away.
💧 Water Loop
Rain → Swales → Pond → Irrigation → Soil
Monsoon water captured and held on the land rather than lost to sheet flow.
👥 Visitor Loop
Experience → Revenue → Reinvestment
Agritourism revenue funds system improvement. Improved systems attract more guests.
🎪
GeoDome Integration
Events + Greenhouse
▾
  • 50' dome sited using monsoon water flow data from Phase 1 observation
  • Storage container windbreak positioned after understanding prevailing wind patterns
  • Greenhouse function tied into nursery and compost feedback loop
  • Events space positioned as premium agritourism experience tier
  • Sculpture garden integrated with ecology loop walking path
📋
Grant Opportunities
USDA + State Programs
▾
  • EQIP — 50% cost-share on water harvesting earthworks. Must file before any earthwork begins.
  • REAP — 25% grant on solar installation. File before ordering panels.
  • AZ Specialty Crop Block Grant — Nursery qualifies. Geographically appropriate plants and specialty fruit.
  • VAPG Planning — Up to $75,000 for value-added product development (soil products, specialty crops).
  • SARE — On-farm research grant for documenting the polyculture system.
I Write These Grant writing is part of my background. Happy to research and write applications for any of these programs — the EQIP application in particular should happen before Phase 2 earthworks begin.
